About This Project
A Localized Framework for Gulf Conflict Management

This dataset codes Gulf Arab state mediation at the episode level, applying the Bercovitch-Gartner (2006) contingency framework alongside Gulf-specific variables developed to capture features of non-Western, interested-state mediation that mainstream frameworks do not address.

The central argument: Gulf states mediate differently from each other because their tool selection and recognition strategies are determined by their distinct relational portfolios, not by generic soft power ambitions.

Empirical Analysis — Pre-Publication

Key Findings from the Dataset

Ten empirically grounded findings drawn from 91 coded Gulf mediation episodes. Each finding tests or extends Bercovitch-Gartner (2006) hypotheses for non-Western, interested-state mediators.

01

Gulf Directive Mediation Does Not Produce What B-G Predicts

82% of Directive episodes produce Access-Transactional outcomes

Bercovitch-Gartner predict Directive strategies produce ceasefires and full settlements in high-intensity conflicts. In the Gulf data, 18 of 22 Directive episodes produce Access-Transactional outcomes instead. Gulf Directive mediation means prisoner releases and POW exchanges, not coerced settlements. H2A and H2B are disconfirmed for Gulf mediators.

Implication: Gulf states lack the enforcement capacity that makes Directive mediation effective in Western/UN contexts. Their leverage is relational, not coercive.
02

Procedural Hosting Produces Formal Agreements in Lower-Intensity Conflicts

9 of 13 Full Settlements come from Procedural episodes

H2D is confirmed: Procedural strategies produce Full and Partial Settlements predominantly in Low and Medium intensity conflicts. The Doha Agreement (Lebanon 2008), Taif Agreement (Lebanon 1989), and Chad-Sudan 2009 are all Procedural hosting cases in lower-stakes political conflicts. Procedural works when both parties want Saudi or Qatari legitimation.

Implication: Hosting capacity and pan-Arab legitimacy are prerequisites for Procedural success, not just strategic choice.

04

The Detainee Channel Is the Most Outcome-Consistent Tool in the Dataset

100% of detainee channel episodes (N=14) produce Access-Transactional

No other mediation tool produces a single outcome category at 100%. Hosting/Formulation produces the highest rate of Full Settlement. The tool deployed predicts the outcome more cleanly than the B-G strategy category. HG5 is confirmed: Gulf mediation is tool-based diplomacy. The instrument chosen tells you what the outcome will be before talks begin.

Implication: Understanding Gulf mediation requires tool-level analysis, not just strategy-level categorisation.

H3 confirmed for same-actor sequences. When a different Gulf state previously succeeded in the same conflict, outcomes are significantly weaker. Relational capital is state-specific and non-transferable. HG3 confirmed. The Yemen sequence (Qatar 2008, Saudi 2011, Oman 2015-2025, Saudi 2023) illustrates the gap precisely.

Implication: Gulf mediation handoffs do not work without deliberate coordination. Substitution is not the same as succession.
06

Humanitarian and Political Tracks Are Parallel Systems

58% of all episodes do not sequence toward political settlement

Of 91 episodes, 53 have sequencing_to_political = No. Humanitarian access objectives produce Access-Transactional outcomes across all conflict intensity levels. The distribution is flat with respect to intensity, meaning humanitarian tools are not stepping stones. HG4 confirmed: Gulf humanitarian mediation is a self-contained mode, not a gateway to political resolution.

Workshop implication: Expecting Gulf humanitarian mediation to evolve naturally into political settlement is a category error. The HDP Nexus requires deliberate design, not organic sequencing.
07

Gulf States Operate Across the Full Conflict Intensity Spectrum

73% of episodes involve high-intensity conflicts

Bercovitch-Gartner predict regional actors concentrate in low-intensity conflicts, leaving high-intensity cases to major powers and international organisations. Gulf states disconfirm this: 66 of 91 episodes are high-intensity. Qatar mediates US-Taliban; Oman holds US-Iran channels; the UAE facilitates Russia-Ukraine POW exchanges. Relational access, not coercive power, explains this anomaly.

Implication: B-G's mediator-intensity hierarchy requires revision for contexts where relational access substitutes for leverage.

Qatar uses the widest tool repertoire, explaining its structural indispensability. Oman uses Backchannel and Detainee channel almost exclusively. Saudi Arabia concentrates 67% of episodes in Hosting/Formulation. UAE concentrates in Quiet facilitation and Detainee channel. These patterns are stable across decades, not case-by-case choices.

Implication: The four mediation postures emerge from the data, not from assertion. Gulf states cannot simply choose different tools.
09

Relational Basis Determines Strategy Selection

Strategic equidistance (Oman) predicts Facilitate Communications in 100% of episodes. Economic leverage (UAE) predicts Directive at 58%. Pan-Arab kinship (Saudi Arabia) predicts Procedural at majority rate. Ideological proximity (Qatar) produces a mixed Directive/Procedural profile reflecting Qatar's broader portfolio. Strategy is not freely chosen. It is downstream of the relational assets each state holds going into a conflict.

Implication: Mediation capacity is relational before it is strategic. A Gulf state cannot credibly deploy a tool its portfolio does not support.

Mediation Posture
Qatar
The Engaged Guarantor

Qatar is the only Gulf state that deploys all four recognition management modes, making it structurally indispensable in conflicts involving non-state actors that other mediators cannot reach. Its relational basis combines ideological proximity to groups like Hamas and the Taliban with a US security architecture that gives it unique leverage as a bridge between adversaries and Washington.

Mediation Posture
Oman
The Invisible Interlocutor

Oman operates on strategic equidistance as its relational basis, deliberately maintaining working relationships with all sides of conflicts that larger Gulf states cannot approach. Its backchannel specialisation generates high rates of transactional and access outcomes, not because Oman seeks agreements, but because it is designed to keep channels open when everything else has broken down.

Mediation Posture
Saudi Arabia
The Hierarchical Host

Saudi Arabia mediates through formal hosting processes, using its pan-Arab legitimacy and regional status to bring state-level parties to Riyadh or Jeddah. Its mediation posture is anchored in Normalise recognition management and Procedural strategy, making it highly effective in lower-intensity political conflicts but structurally limited in conflicts involving non-state actors it refuses to engage directly.

Mediation Posture
United Arab Emirates
The Results Broker

The UAE deploys mediation as a results-oriented, commercially intelligent tool. Economic leverage is its primary relational basis, enabling it to conduct discrete, visible transactions in contexts where political commitment would be too costly. The Russia-Ukraine POW exchange series is the paradigm: six separate exchanges, each producing a concrete humanitarian outcome, none sequencing toward political settlement.
